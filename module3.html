Module 3: Child Safety

Welcome to Child Safety

This module explains how Family Church seeks to create a child safe and child friendly culture where children and young people are respected, valued and protected from harm.

You will:

  • learn the church’s child safety commitments
  • understand your role in protecting children and young people
  • review reporting, complaints and safe practice expectations
  • apply the policy in practical situations

Child safety is not only a policy requirement. It is part of the culture, leadership and daily practice of the church.

Referenced policies: Child Safe Policy; Code of Conduct

Our Child Safety Commitment

As a child safe organisation, Cairns Family Church commits to creating a child safe and child friendly culture and environment where all children and young people are respected, valued and encouraged to reach their full potential.

  • promote child wellbeing
  • prevent harm to children and young people
  • build safe culture through leadership, governance and practice
  • embed child safety into church life and ministry

Child safety is everyone’s responsibility.

Referenced policies: Child Safe Policy

Leadership, Governance and Culture

The Child Safe Policy states that child safety and wellbeing must be embedded in organisational leadership, governance and culture.

  • the policy and code of conduct form a public commitment to child safety
  • leadership is expected to champion and model a child safe culture
  • risk management processes focus on preventing, identifying and reducing risk
  • annual child protection training supports understanding of obligations, information sharing and record keeping

Volunteers help build this culture through everyday decisions and conduct.

Referenced policies: Child Safe Policy; Code of Conduct

Children’s Rights and Participation

Children and young people should be informed, heard and taken seriously.

  • children are encouraged to express views and make suggestions
  • peer support and friendships are encouraged through age-appropriate programs and groups
  • staff and volunteers should create welcoming, child friendly environments
  • children must have opportunities to raise concerns and be taken seriously

A safe church is one where children know they matter and know how to speak up.

Referenced policies: Child Safe Policy

Families and Community Matter

Child safety includes partnership with families and the wider community.

  • families are encouraged to participate in decisions affecting their child
  • open communication supports safe ministry
  • permission forms help identify relevant safety information such as medical needs, special needs and custody arrangements
  • families may inspect relevant policies and procedures when required

Good communication supports protection, trust and accountability.

Referenced policies: Child Safe Policy

Equity, Inclusion and Diverse Needs

The church commits to anticipating children’s diverse circumstances and responding effectively to those with additional vulnerabilities.

  • programs should accommodate varied needs
  • children must have access to information, support and complaints processes that are culturally safe and easy to understand
  • inclusion is part of safe practice
  • children should be treated with dignity and respect

Child safety includes cultural safety, accessibility and inclusion.

Referenced policies: Child Safe Policy; Code of Conduct

Suitable People and Safe Recruitment

People working with children must be suitable and supported.

  • the recruitment, screening and training of employees and volunteers emphasises child safety
  • all persons 18 years and older who work or volunteer with children must hold a current Blue Card
  • induction includes responsibilities, record keeping, information sharing and reporting obligations
  • ongoing supervision and people management focus on child safety and wellbeing

Suitability is not assumed. It is actively assessed and maintained.

Referenced policies: Child Safe Policy; Screening Policy

Training and Awareness

Child safety requires ongoing education and training.

  • orientation and annual child safe training are required
  • training includes indicators of child harm, including harm caused by other children and young people
  • training explains how to respond effectively to child safety concerns and allegations
  • training also supports culturally safe environments for children and young people

Training is part of keeping people alert, informed and accountable.

Referenced policies: Child Safe Policy; Screening Policy

Safe Physical and Online Environments

Child safety includes the environments where ministry takes place.

  • risk management must reduce opportunities for harm
  • safe practices include supervision, sign in and sign out, toileting, illness, first aid, emergency evacuation, transport and photography/video procedures
  • official uses of online environments must take the code of conduct and child safe policy into account
  • third party services and facilities should also be selected and managed with child safety in mind

Being welcoming is not enough. Environments must also be actively safe.

Referenced policies: Child Safe Policy; Code of Conduct

Responding to Concerns and Complaints

The policy requires processes that are child focused and accessible.

  • complaints should be taken seriously and responded to promptly and thoroughly
  • children and families should be able to understand the complaints process
  • reporting procedures must address legal, privacy and employment obligations
  • illegal activity must be reported to relevant authorities

Children need processes that are safe, understandable and responsive.

Referenced policies: Child Safe Policy; Privacy Policy

When Harm Is Suspected or Disclosed

If there is a reasonable suspicion of abuse, harm or risk of harm, action must be taken.

  • reasonable suspicion is enough — proof is not required
  • if a child discloses harm, do not promise secrecy
  • reassure the child that it is not their fault
  • do not ask probing questions
  • report the matter as soon as possible in line with church and legal requirements
  • record the report using the required form
Child protection is everyone’s responsibility.

Referenced policies: Child Safe Policy; Code of Conduct

Queensland Reporting Expectations

The policy highlights important Queensland reporting obligations.

  • where there is reason to believe a child is experiencing harm, at risk of harm or being neglected, mandated reporters must report to Child Safety Services
  • if a child is at immediate risk, call 000
  • it is also an offence for an adult in Queensland not to report sexual offending against a child by another adult to police

This means volunteers must take child safety concerns seriously and escalate them promptly through the correct channels.

Referenced policies: Child Safe Policy

Known Offenders and Persons of Concern

The policy recognises that child safety also includes managing risk from known offenders and persons of concern.

  • strict risk management, monitoring and mentoring may apply
  • boundaries can be formalised through an agreement
  • support for spiritual growth may still be provided where appropriate
  • the safety of children remains the priority

This area is handled through senior leadership and board oversight, not informal volunteer decisions.

Referenced policies: Child Safe Policy; Screening Policy

Review and Continuous Improvement

Child safety is not static. The policy requires regular review and improvement.

  • child safe practices are reviewed through audits, debriefs, complaints and incident reports
  • feedback from staff, volunteers, children, families and the community informs improvement
  • findings and improvements should be communicated appropriately
  • policies and procedures should be clear, easy to understand and supported by training

Safe organisations keep learning and improving.
